# PART 2 — THE TENSION
### "The growth is real. But it's concentrated in a very small slice of customers."

---

**What you're showing:** Five numbers that reveal the quality problem hiding behind the growth.

---

## The story to tell

> "Take the same 2.9 million users. Same data. Different lens."

> "**58.5% of users placed exactly one order.** Ever. 1.7 million people came, bought once, and never came back. That's not a customer. That's a transaction."

> "**57.8% of users — nearly the same group — only ever bought during a sale.** They have never placed an order at full price. Never engaged on a BAU day. They didn't find Tira. A sale found them."

[These two numbers often overlap heavily — most one-time buyers are also sale-only buyers. Makes the problem more concentrated, not less.]

> "Now look at who these people are when they first arrive. **36.8% of first-time buyers spent less than ₹1,000 on their first order.** 6.8% spent less than ₹500. 30% spent between ₹500 and ₹999. These are not the customers who build a platform's future."

> "And when you look at all users on the platform — not just first-timers — **31.8% have an average ASP below ₹500.** Consistently buying the cheapest products. That is not a premium beauty platform number."

**Then land the fifth number. This is the most important one.**

> "But here's the one that keeps me up at night."

> "We have 247,000 users who have placed 6 or more orders. These are our loyalists. Our best customers. The people the platform was built for."

> "**43% of them are spending less per item than when they started.**"

> "Not a little less. Their average selling price has gone from ₹1,401 to ₹769. That's a **38.5% drop in ASP** over their lifetime on the platform. We kept them. We lost their wallet share."

> "The platform is retaining loyal customers — and simultaneously training them to spend less."

---

**Key numbers for this slide:**
- 58.5% — one-time buyers (1,697,231 users)
- 57.8% — sale-only buyers (never purchased at BAU)
- 36.8% — first-time buyers who spent less than ₹1,000
- 31.8% — users with average ASP below ₹500
- 43% — of 6+ order users are downgrading (ASP: ₹1,401 → ₹769, -38.5%)

**Flip side — the 23.3%:**
- Users who buy across both BAU and sale events: 676,873 (23.3%)
- They average 5.8 orders — vs 1.5 for sale-only buyers
- This 23.3% is the platform's health core. Everything else is noise.

# PART 3 — THE INSIGHTS
### "Here is what the data says about customers who do cross the loyalty threshold — and what gets them there."

---

**What you're showing:** Five findings that diagnose why loyalty works when it works, and where it breaks.

---

## Insight 1 — The Lifecycle Is Real (Finding 1)

> "First, let's prove that the product works. Because it does."

> "Customers who stay on the platform increase their average order value by **59%** across their first 30 orders. They start at ₹1,667. By orders 26 to 30, they're spending ₹2,651. Their ASP grows too — from ₹928 to ₹1,247. They are genuinely trading up over time."

> "They also explore broadly at first — buying from close to 2 different brands per order in orders 7 to 10 — and then they narrow down into a repertoire. That's normal loyalty behaviour. Discovery phase, then commitment phase."

> "The lifecycle is real. The problem is getting people into it."

[Only 10,876 users have reached orders 26–30. The curve exists — but the funnel to reach it is leaky.]

---

## Insight 2 — The ₹1,000 Threshold (Finding 2)

> "So what is the biggest lever to get someone into that lifecycle?"

> "First-order value."

> "Users who spend between ₹500 and ₹999 on their first order have a **34.5%** chance of coming back. Users who cross ₹1,000 on their first order? **43.7%**. That is a **9.2 percentage point jump** at a single price band crossing."

> "The sweet spot is ₹3,000 to ₹4,999 — 51.9% retention. Above ₹5,000, it actually drops slightly to 50.6%. Those are gifting and occasion buyers. They spent big once — they're not coming back for themselves."

> "The single most actionable acquisition lever on this platform is getting a new user above ₹1,000 on their first visit. Everything else flows from that."

---

## Insight 3 — The Event Type Paradox (Finding 3)

> "Now here's something that will probably surprise you."

> "We looked at what event a user was shopping during when they placed their first order — and how that predicted their retention."

> "VIP Day — our most exclusive event — delivers **60.2% retention**. Best on the platform. Average first-order AOV of ₹1,757. These buyers come back."

> "1-Day Sale — our flash sale format — delivers **₹1,841 average first-order AOV**. Highest first spend of any event. And a **32.8% retention rate**. Worst on the platform."

> "The highest-spending first-time buyer is our least loyal customer. That is the 1-Day Sale paradox. They paid premium — and they will never come back. Because they didn't buy because they love Tira. They bought because the discount was good."

> "VIP Day, by the way, reaches only 5,003 users. That's 0.17% of our total user base. The best event we run is barely running."

[BAU: 40.5% retention, 808K users. P0 Sale: 42.0%, 675K users. These are the volume acquisition channels. Neither is great, but both are better than flash sales.]

---

## Insight 4 — The Return Window (Finding 9)

> "For users who do place a second order — when they come back matters enormously."

> "Users who return within 15 to 30 days of their first order have a **63.8% probability of placing a third order**. That is the peak."

> "Users who take more than 180 days? **46.4%**. That's a 17-point drop in just-add-time."

> "264,000 users have crossed the 180-day mark right now. They placed a second order. They haven't placed a third. Every day they wait, the odds of them coming back drop."

> "The Day 15 push is the single highest-leverage CRM trigger on this platform. Not day 7 — that's too early, most users haven't even decided yet. Not day 30 — that's starting to be too late. Day 15. That is the window."

---

## Insight 5 — Cohort Quality Is Declining (Finding 8)

> "Last one. And this is the structural one."

> "In January 2024, 62.6% of new users placed a second order. That was our benchmark cohort."

> "By July 2024, that number was 49.8%. By January 2025 — 44%. By July 2025 — 37.2%."

> "At the same time, the average first-order AOV of new users has been going up. From ₹1,632 in January 2024 to ₹1,817 in recent cohorts."

> "So we are acquiring users who spend more on their first visit — and who are increasingly less likely to come back. We are getting higher-value transactions from lower-intent customers."

> "That is the core diagnosis. The platform's acquisition mix has shifted toward promotional, high-spend, low-intent buyers. The growth numbers look good. The cohort quality is quietly eroding."

[Note: Jan–Mar 2026 cohort numbers (25.5%, 20%, 12.8%) are partly a data timing artifact — those users haven't had enough time to repeat yet. But the 2024 decay is real and structural — those cohorts have had 12–24 months to repeat.]

---

**Supporting data — Brand Retention (Finding 5):**

> "One more thing worth naming. Not all brands are equal retention engines."

> "Our best retaining brands — Aminu, Thank You Farmer, First Aid Beauty, Shiseido, Klairs — are returning 60–70% of first-time buyers. Korean skincare brands specifically are a cluster: Klairs (59.8%), Thank You Farmer (67.9%), ONE THING (58.5%), Mixsoon (57.9%). Accessible price point, genuinely loyal customers, strong platform fit."

> "Our worst retaining brands: Bombay Shaving Company (15.3%), AGARO (16%), Beardo (19%). All men's grooming. Beardo alone brings in 28,000 first-time users a year who don't come back. That's a category misfit — and it's dragging our platform retention averages down."

# PART 4 — RECOMMENDATIONS
### "Six moves. All aimed at the same thing — converting volume into value."

---

**Rec 1 — Protect the ₹1,000 threshold**

> "The single biggest retention lever is crossing ₹1,000 on the first order. We have 869,000 users in the ₹500–999 band. If we can nudge half of them across ₹1,000 — through bundle prompts, guided discovery, cross-sell at checkout — that's roughly 4 to 5 percentage points of platform retention. That's not a small number."

**Rec 2 — Scale VIP Day. Retire 1-Day Flash Sales.**

> "VIP Day delivers our best customers. 60.2% retention. We run it for 5,000 people. We need to run it for 50,000. Meanwhile, 1-Day Sale is actively creating a non-loyal user base at scale. The economics look good upfront — AOV ₹1,841. The LTV is a disaster. Flash sales should be reframed as loyalty-member exclusives, not mass acquisition tools."

**Rec 3 — Make Day 15 a platform-wide CRM moment**

> "Shift the CRM team's north star metric from second-order rate to second-order-within-30-days rate. Those are not the same thing. A user who comes back on day 60 is significantly less likely to reach a third order than one who comes back on day 20. Day 15, personalised by first-order brand and category, is the trigger. Build it, automate it, measure it."

**Rec 4 — Intervene on the downgraders**

> "107,000 loyal users — people who have ordered 6 or more times — are spending 38.5% less per item than when they started. These are not disengaged users. They're active. But they've drifted to cheaper SKUs. Re-introduce them to premium through editorial, curated edits, and sampling. Do not use discounts. Discounts will accelerate the downgrade cycle."

**Rec 5 — Fix the men's grooming drain**

> "Beardo, Bombay Shaving Company, AGARO — these three brands collectively bring in tens of thousands of users per year who almost never return. 15 to 19% retention. Tira is a premium beauty platform. Men's grooming in its current form is a category misfit. Either deprioritize acquisition spend on these brands, or build a proper men's premium pathway that actually retains them."

**Rec 6 — Build Korean skincare as a franchise**

> "This is the opportunity, not the problem. Korean skincare — Klairs, Thank You Farmer, ONE THING, Mixsoon, COSRX, SKIN1004 — delivers the best combination of volume and loyalty on the platform. Build it as a visible franchise. K-Beauty editorial hub, curated bundles, dedicated CRM journey. This is both an acquisition lever and a retention lever."

## THE CLOSING LINE

> "So — are we building customers or buying orders?"

> "Both. But right now, for 57.8% of the platform, we are buying orders. The infrastructure to build customers is there — the lifecycle works, the data is clear, the levers are identified. We just need to aim the platform at the right kind of growth."

> "The 23.3% who engage across both BAU and sales events average 5.8 orders. The 57.8% who only buy in sales average 1.5. That gap is the entire opportunity."
